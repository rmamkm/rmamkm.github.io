Notable Leaders of the Civil War

The chain of command during the American Civil War is composed of many men who will be remembered for significant contributions to the modernization of diplomacy, warfare, transportaion and medicine. The lives of these leaders, from Lincoln to Stonewall Jackson would quickly become legend.

  • Union Army
  • Confederate Army

Abraham Lincoln

16th President of the United States of America

March 4th, 1861 to April 15th, 1865

Lincoln, best known for his leadership the United States during one of its most turbulent times and for the historic abolishment of slavery. He issued the Emancipation Proclamation on January 1, 1863 which declared the freedom of slaves in the Confederate states

Lincoln died April 15th, 1865--just 6 days following the Confederate surrender. John Wikes Booth, a Confederate sympathizer and spy, was Lincoln's assassin and was captured and killed 12 days later.

Ulysses S. Grant

Commanding General

Perhaps the most famous military leader of the civil war, Grant was well received and respected. Following his leadership success of the Union Army, Grant was elected the 18th President of the United States.

William Tecumseh Sherman

Major General

Greatly respected by both Lincoln and Grant, Sherman was entrusted with command of the Western Theatre. His campaigns are well-regarded as the basis of modern warfare. Beginning with Grant's Presidency, Sherman was promoted to Commanding General.

George Henry Thomas

Major General

Like many southern military leaders, Thomas was an experienced soldier. However, at the onset of war, he remained loyal to the US Army, which was a point of contention with his adversaries. He was integral to Union success given his command of battlefield tactics.

George Gordon Meade

Major General

Know for his temper, "Old Snapping Turtle" led the Army of the Potomac and was responsible for many of the Union's key victories including the Battle of Gettysburg.

Jefferson Davis

President of the Confederate States of America

February 18th, 1861 to May 10th, 1865

A well experienced leader, Davis served the United States as a Congressman, Sentor and Secretary of War. Like many of his Generals, Davis served in the United States military and had significant strategic and tactical experience in preceding wars.

Robert E. Lee

General-in-chief

One of the most prominent leaders in the War was Robert E. Lee. Respected by both his men and his adversaries, Lee gained notability for his support of Reconstruction and general efforts to reintegrate the South.

Patrick Cleburne

Major General

Born in Ireland and enlisting without prior military experience, Cleburne was an anomaly. He rose from private to Major General in 2 years and was often held unpopular views, including arming slaves in exchanged for their freedom.

Stonewall Jackson

Lieutenant General

Thomas Jonathan Jackson was given his well-known moniker for his actions in the the first battle of Manassas. Jackson was key leader for much of the South's inital successes and was often used by Lee for strategic battles.

Nathan Bedford Forrest

Lieutenant General

A divisive leader, known for his ruthlessness and inhumane treatment on the battlefield. Forrest was known as the "devil" by Union forces and had successfully defeated Union forces twice the size of his forces.
